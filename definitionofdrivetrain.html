Performance Driving: RWD is favored in sports cars and racing for its balanced feel, precise steering, and ability to handle high power.

Sports Cars & Luxury Sedans: For dynamic driving and a premium feel.

Motorsports: Dominant in racing series like Formula Drift and NASCAR for its control.
Considerations: Less Traction on Slippery Surfaces: RWD can struggle in snow, ice, or heavy rain compared to front-wheel or all-wheel drive.

Key Functions & Benefits
Enhanced Traction: By powering all wheels, 4WD prevents single wheels from spinning uselessly, providing grip where one or two wheels might otherwise lose traction.
Improved Control: Distributing power to all wheels gives drivers better control on slippery surfaces like ice, gravel, or sand.
Off-Road Capability: Essential for serious off-roading, allowing vehicles to climb obstacles, cross deep mud, and navigate uneven landscapes.
Increased Torque (Low Range): The low (4L) setting uses lower gear ratios to multiply torque, providing immense pulling power for slow, arduous climbs.

How It Works (Simplified)
Transfer Case: A central component that splits engine power between the front and rear axles.
Part-Time 4WD (Common in Trucks): Allows switching between 2WD (rear-wheel drive for efficiency) and 4WD (4H for high-speed traction, 4L for low-speed torque).
Locked Differentials: Often paired with 4WD, these force both wheels on an axle to turn at the same speed, ensuring power goes to the wheel with grip, even if another wheel is lifted.
When to Use It
Engage for: Mud, deep snow, rocky trails, steep inclines, or when stuck.
Disengage for: Normal paved roads (unless conditions are icy) for better fuel economy and to prevent strain on the drivetrain.
